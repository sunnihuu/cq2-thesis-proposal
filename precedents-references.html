Precedents

Project Drawdown

Project Drawdown identifies food systems as one of the most powerful leverage points for climate action. By linking diet shifts, food waste reduction, and land use to both emissions reduction and carbon removal, Drawdown frames food as a system that connects everyday behavior with planetary-scale climate processes. This project builds on Drawdown's global framework, but shifts the scale of intervention from abstract global metrics to everyday urban life. Rather than targeting planetary targets directly, it asks how food–climate logic can be translated into neighborhood-scale decision-making and behavioral systems.

drawdown.org →

NYC Food and Climate Strategy

The NYC Food and Climate Strategy formally recognizes food as a core component of the city's climate response. The strategy prioritizes reducing food-related emissions, eliminating food waste from landfills, and building a resilient and equitable food system. This project aligns with the Strategy's goals, but operates at a different scale. Instead of focusing on policy targets alone, it prototypes behavioral and neighborhood-level systems that can complement municipal tools and support localized implementation of food–climate policy.

NYC Food and Climate Strategy →

Behavioral Climate Interfaces (Carbon Labeling and Choice Architecture)

A growing body of work explores how information design and behavioral nudging can influence food-related climate decisions, including carbon labeling on menus and choice architecture in food environments. These projects demonstrate that behavior can shift when climate information is made visible at the point of decision. This thesis extends these approaches by embedding behavioral interfaces within a spatial vulnerability framework, rather than treating food choice as an isolated individual action.

Together, these precedents establish the policy, scientific, and behavioral foundations for this project, while highlighting the need for neighborhood-scale operational systems.

Data Sources and Provenance

This project draws on publicly available datasets from NYC Open Data, the American Community Survey, and the NYC Department of Health. These datasets reflect administrative definitions and reporting practices that shape how food vulnerability is measured and represented. Rather than treating data as neutral facts, the project understands them as situated indicators that require interpretation within social, economic, and policy contexts.

The project is data-driven and interface-based and does not rely on physical sensors at this stage.
