1. Multi-Scalar Spatial Analysis

Purpose

To examine how urban food systems operate across global, city, and neighborhood scales.

What was done

  • Mapped food-related indicators including food prices, restaurant density, and household food practices at the city scale.
  • Related neighborhood-level conditions to broader food distribution networks and logistical systems operating beyond the neighborhood.

Why it matters

Situates everyday food practices within larger infrastructural systems rather than treating them as isolated local conditions.

2. Index-Based Exploration of Food Vulnerability

Purpose

To explore how food-related vulnerability and exposure are unevenly distributed across neighborhoods.

What was done

  • Developed an exploratory Food Vulnerability Index combining normalized measures of restaurant density, household food storage capacity, and poverty.
  • Used the index as a comparative and diagnostic tool to surface spatial patterns rather than as a definitive metric.

Why it matters

Surfaces spatial patterns to prompt inquiry instead of ranking neighborhoods or fixing classifications.

3. Interpretive Interface Prototyping

Purpose

To make infrastructural relationships legible and comparable across scales.

What was done

  • Brought together food flows, freight routes, infrastructural concentration, and health exposure within a shared spatial interface.
  • Designed the interface to support interpretation and discussion rather than service delivery or behavior change.

Why it matters

Treats prototyping as a form of inquiry that examines how visual structure and comparison shape understanding of urban climate infrastructure.
