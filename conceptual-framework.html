Conceptual Framework

This project is grounded in the understanding of food as a form of urban climate infrastructure. The framework brings together four interrelated dimensions that shape how food systems operate in cities and how vulnerability emerges.

Carbon

Food systems generate significant greenhouse gas emissions through production, processing, transportation, storage, and waste. At the global scale, food, agriculture, and land use account for roughly one quarter of total emissions, placing food among the core systems driving climate change alongside buildings and transportation.

Infrastructure

In dense cities like New York, food functions as a distributed infrastructure. Daily life depends on continuous flows of goods, energy, labor, and logistics. Disruptions to these flows, whether caused by climate events, economic shocks, or supply chain failures, directly affect access, affordability, and stability.

Inequality

Food systems reflect and reproduce social and economic inequality. Income, housing conditions, and neighborhood infrastructure shape how people obtain, store, and consume food. Vulnerability is not evenly distributed, and certain communities bear disproportionate exposure to food-related risk.

Risk

Climate change increases the frequency and severity of disruptions that affect food systems. Flooding, heat, energy outages, and transportation failures translate into food stress at the household and neighborhood level. Food risk is therefore not only environmental, but also economic and social.

Together, these four dimensions position food not as a background aspect of urban life, but as an active climate system. This framework connects global food–climate research, such as Project Drawdown, with New York City's policy context, including the NYC Food and Climate Strategy. It establishes the basis for analyzing where food vulnerability accumulates spatially and for identifying everyday behavioral decision points as sites of design intervention.
