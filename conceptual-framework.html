This project is grounded in a conceptual framework that understands urban food systems as a form of climate infrastructure. Rather than approaching food systems only through questions of access, consumption, or individual behavior, the framework treats them as interconnected systems that sustain everyday urban life while simultaneously producing uneven environmental and health exposures across the city.

A central premise of the framework is that food related vulnerability is not an isolated neighborhood condition, but the outcome of broader systems operating across multiple scales. Urban food systems are examined at global, city, and neighborhood levels, recognizing that decisions, efficiencies, and flows at larger scales often shape localized conditions of exposure. Processes such as food logistics, cold chain operations, regulatory structures, and infrastructural concentration play a significant role in determining where environmental and health burdens are experienced.

The framework brings together three closely related dimensions. First, it considers how urban food systems operate across multiple spatial scales, linking global supply chains and citywide distribution networks to neighborhood level conditions. Second, it emphasizes the uneven distribution of benefits and burdens embedded within these systems, where citywide food availability and affordability depend on infrastructural systems that concentrate emissions, noise, and air pollution in specific communities. Third, it introduces interpretive tools that help make these relationships visible and understandable for audiences engaged in community advocacy and planning.

Within this conceptual structure, design and visualization are positioned as modes of inquiry rather than solution delivery. Instead of prescribing behavioral change or proposing singular interventions, the framework uses visual and spatial tools to clarify how food systems function as climate infrastructure and how their impacts are distributed. By situating neighborhood level exposure within broader food and climate systems, the framework supports more informed discussion, interpretation, and advocacy around urban climate resilience.
