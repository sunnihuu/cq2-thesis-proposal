Sunni Hu

Columbia University GSAPP       Computational Design Practice        Colloquium 2       Fall 2025

This thesis investigates Hunts Point in the Bronx as a critical site where New York City’s food system, climate impacts, and environmental health intersect. As home to the city’s primary food distribution center, Hunts Point plays a central role in supplying food to millions of New Yorkers, while simultaneously absorbing disproportionate environmental and health burdens associated with logistics, cold storage, and freight transportation.

The project examines how food-related carbon emissions, pollution exposure, and health impacts remain largely invisible within everyday urban decision-making and planning frameworks, even in areas that have received sustained investment and long-term redevelopment efforts. Rather than treating food as a matter of individual choice or access alone, the thesis frames food systems as urban climate infrastructure whose benefits and costs are unevenly distributed across neighborhoods.

Through community-grounded research and human-centered design, the project aims to develop a community-facing urban technology prototype that translates complex food, carbon, and infrastructure data into legible spatial and narrative interfaces. The prototype is conceived as a visibility and leverage tool, intended to help community members and other stakeholders better understand how citywide food consumption flows through Hunts Point, where environmental burdens concentrate, and how these patterns relate to climate resilience and public health.

The project will explore how alternative forms of data representation, interaction, and storytelling can support collective understanding and more equitable forms of urban climate resilience. By centering Hunts Point as both infrastructure and community, the thesis contributes a critical design perspective on how urban technologies can surface hidden systems and challenge the limits of existing planning metrics.
