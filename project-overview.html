What if everyday eating choices could become a visible, data-driven, and actionable lever for building urban food and climate resilience in New York City?

New York Cityâ€™s food system sits at the intersection of climate risk, social vulnerability, and everyday behavior. While food consumption significantly contributes to carbon emissions, food access and affordability are unevenly distributed across neighborhoods, often reinforcing existing inequalities. These challenges are typically addressed at policy or infrastructure scales, leaving individual behavior disconnected from systemic change.

This project investigates how everyday eating can become an entry point for urban climate resilience. By mapping food-related vulnerability (such as high consumption density, low household storage capacity, and poverty) across NYC, the project reveals where food systems are most fragile. It proposes a data-driven behavioral interface that translates urban food and climate data into actionable insights, enabling residents, communities, and institutions to make informed, low-carbon food choices that collectively support a more resilient urban food system.
