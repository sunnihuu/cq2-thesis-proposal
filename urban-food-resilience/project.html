What is at stake

Urban food systems face increasing pressure from climate change, supply chain disruption, and spatial constraints. Food vulnerability intersects with neighborhood inequality, creating cascading risks to public health and economic stability. This project examines how cities, particularly New York, can develop more resilient and low-carbon food networks by treating food as both critical infrastructure and a climate system.

The research connects consumption patterns, distribution networks, and production methods to explore how redesigning food systems can advance urban climate resilience while addressing food justice.

Scales & dimensions

The project operates across multiple scales:

  • Individual: Consumer behavior, eating patterns, and carbon awareness
  • Neighborhood: Food access, vulnerability indices, and community infrastructure
  • City: Infrastructure networks, policy frameworks, and systemic resilience

Spatial analysis reveals how food vulnerability correlates with transportation networks, land use, and socioeconomic conditions across NYC's neighborhoods.

Planned outputs

  • Interactive atlas maps showing food vulnerability and opportunity
  • Computational analysis tools for food system assessment
  • Design interventions and scenario visualizations
  • Policy recommendations and implementation frameworks
  • Case studies from global food resilience precedents
